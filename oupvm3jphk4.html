Does HETG/HRC-I work?

The observation of Capella was successful. Data can be taken with the HETG and the HRC-I as a detector. However, more work, both in calibration and in CIAO, is needed before this mode can be used routinely. At the current time, it is only warrented for a few special cases where the LEG resolving power is insufficient, but emission lines in the range 15-25 Å are crucial. On the other hand, with increasing contamination there will be more and more cases where the HRC can offer a better overall performance than ACIS-S.

The good news: What we can learn from absorption

In this section, I want to highlight two recent scientific results that are based on absorption line spectroscopy using HETG data. Obviously, picking out just two results from the recent literature means that much other important work is not shown here. Both examples re-analyze archival HETG data for their studies, looking at aspects in the data that were not central to the original observation. The data archive of existing HETG observations is quite rich already and grows with every new cycle. As the HETG instrument team, we try to make it as easy as we can to look at archival data. That is why we maintain TGCat (http://www.tgcat.mit.edu), a web-archive of all Chandra grating data that offers quicklook images of all reduced datasets and downloads of science-ready data products.
\citealt{2017RAA....17...95F} present a new study of the bright Seyfert I galaxy NGC 3783. This target has accumulated more than 1 Ms of exposure time with Chandra/HETG and a number of works have fitted the absorption lines seen in the spectrum to derive the properties of the warm absorbers seen in the spectrum - with sometimes inconsistent results. What is new about the work of  Fu et al. (2017) is that they simultaneously fit X-ray data from Chandra/HETG and UV data from HST/COS (both observations were taken within a few days of each other) using the same model in XSTAR. Their model provides a good fit to both the UV and the X-ray data at the same time, using a total of five warm absorbers. Only two of these seem to be in pressure balance; two seem to be smaller clouds that have only recently been blown out from the torus. The authors estimate a total mass outflow of 0.2-4 solar masses per year.
The second example of HETG absorption spectroscopy that I want to highlight here looks at absorption between us and low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXB). \citet{2016ApJ...827...49S} analyze HETG spectra towards several galactic LMXBs and in particular study the Si edge at 1.844 keV.  There are several features near that edge which are caused by Si that is not free in atomic form, but where the energy of the edge is shifted because of ionization or because the Si is bound in dust grains.  The edge feature is also measurable broadened due to the turbulent velocity of the gas by a few hundred km/s. The high turbulence and the ionization state indicate that most of the absorbing mass is located close to the LMXBs. In addition, Schulz et al. find that the edge structure can be variable on the time scale of days as the Si responds to changing ionization from the LMXB.

Summary

The HETG team is actively investigating observations with HETG/HRC-I. We hope that this combination can be useful for observers who want to use the HETG, but also require good signal between 15 and 25 Å. I presented some promising initial work, but I have to highlight that, as of now, this is not an officially supported mode for general observers and that the current CIAO software will not process these observations out-of-the-box.
